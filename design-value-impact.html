Section 01

How the design value conversation happens inside organizations

The conversation about design's value rarely begins as a philosophical inquiry. It is triggered by specific organizational pressures: a new design leader seeking headcount, a CFO questioning budgets during austerity, competitive anxiety when a design-led rival captures market share, or most commonly since 2023, layoff cycles that force every function to justify its existence.

McKinsey found that fewer than 5% of companies could make objective design decisions, and in most organizations the question of design's worth remains stuck in middle management, rarely rising to the C-suite.

The conversation typically follows a predictable arc. Design enters an organization as a downstream execution function. Peter Merholz, co-author of Org Design for Design Orgs, calls this the "Trojan horse" effect: organizations bring design in because they need screens built, and this becomes the understood limit of design's contribution.

The value conversation gets triggered when design leaders push for upstream involvement. At this point, business stakeholders ask the uncomfortable question: "What's the return on this investment?"

  1. Design leaders initiate it when seeking resources or organizational elevation.
  2. Finance and operations executives force it during budget cycles or restructuring, demanding accountability in business language.
  3. Product and engineering leaders shape it by defining whether design is a peer function or a service bureau.

EY's 2024 survey found that over 60% of design leaders felt unsupported by C-suite partners, citing unclear expectations and limited strategic involvement.

The frameworks used vary by organizational maturity. Early-stage conversations rely on portfolio storytelling. More mature organizations adopt formal measurement frameworks. The most sophisticated use balanced scorecards combining financial metrics (conversion, revenue attribution), customer metrics (NPS, CSAT, task success), process metrics (time-to-market, rework reduction), and learning metrics (team capability, research coverage).

Section 02

The major frameworks for measuring design's business impact

McKinsey Design Index

McKinsey's 2018 "Business Value of Design" report tracked 300 publicly listed companies over five years, analyzing more than 2 million financial data points and 100,000+ design actions. It identified 12 design actions clustered into four themes:

Analytical leadershipDesign treated as a top-management issue with measurable outcomes
Cross-functional talentMaking design everyone's responsibility, not a siloed team
User experienceBreaking barriers between physical, digital, and service design
Continuous iterationTesting prototypes with real users early and often

Companies needed strength across all four themes. The market disproportionately rewarded top performers, while differences between second, third, and fourth quartiles were marginal.

Design Value Index

The Design Management Institute's Design Value Index tracked stock prices of design-centric companies (Apple, IBM, Nike, P&G, Starbucks) against the S&P 500 over ten-year periods. Design-led companies outperformed by 211–228%. The UK Design Council found 61 design-led brands outperformed the FTSE 100 by over 200%.

InVision's maturity model

InVision's 2018 "New Design Frontier" surveyed 2,200+ organizations across 77 countries.

Key finding

41% of companies sit at Level 1 (Producers). Only 5% reach Level 5 (Visionaries). Team size does not predict maturity. Design-mature companies showed 5x greater revenue impact and 26x greater impact on company valuation.

Forrester's research

The most rigorous Forrester work is the Total Economic Impact study of IBM Design Thinking (2018), finding 301% ROI, $36.3 million net present value, and 50% reduction in time-to-market. A broader 2020 model found mature design thinking practices deliver 85%+ ROI.

Section 03

What design can steal from product management, DevOps, and QA

Product management: the closest parallel

PM's legitimacy came from three moves: establishing an independent reporting line, developing a shared vocabulary around business metrics (MRR, ARR, NPS, churn, DAU/MAU), and positioning itself as the bridge between business, technology, and user needs.

PM's most transferable concept for design is the North Star Metric. PM also mastered leading and lagging indicator chains:

Research-informed decisions (leading) → better product-market fit (intermediate) → reduced churn (lagging) → revenue growth (lagging)

DevOps: the discipline that built its own scorecard

The DORA framework distilled everything into four metrics: deployment frequency, lead time for changes, change failure rate, and mean time to recovery.

"Traditionally, IT has been viewed as a cost center. However, IT done right is a value driver and innovation engine."

DORA / Google Cloud
The design implication

Design lacks its DORA equivalent. A "DORA for Design" might measure: design velocity (iteration speed), design adoption (% of recommendations implemented), experience quality (task success, satisfaction), and design debt (cost of rework from insufficient design investment).

QA's "cost of quality" model

Fixing a defect during design costs 1x, during implementation 6.5x, during testing 15x, and after release 60–100x. Philip Crosby's maxim "quality is free" translates directly to design.

The universal pattern: five strategies

  1. Language shift from outputs to outcomes Every successful function stopped measuring what it produced and started measuring what it changed.
  2. The "pain first" strategy Jared Spool (UX), QA (cost of defects), DevOps (cost of downtime) all lead with the cost of the problem. Loss aversion is psychologically stronger than gain-seeking.
  3. Standardized metric sets DevOps converged on 4 DORA metrics. Marketing converged on CAC, LTV, ROI. Design's lack of consensus metrics remains its biggest organizational liability.
  4. Dual-axis arguments DevOps argues speed AND stability. Design should argue innovation AND consistency, or speed AND quality.
  5. The maturity curve Every function moves from ad hoc → emerging → structured → strategic. The inflection point: getting an independent seat at the leadership table.
Section 04

The hard problem: demonstrating upstream and strategic design value

The hard problem is demonstrating the value of discovery design, design research, service design, and strategic design, which happens upstream, far from the point of revenue.

Why upstream design is inherently harder to measure

  1. Temporal distance Upstream work happens months or years before measurable outcomes.
  2. Causal diffusion Strategic design influences dozens of subsequent decisions, none solely attributable to it.
  3. Counterfactual invisibility You cannot measure the disaster that didn't happen or the wrong product that wasn't built.
  4. Multi-actor contribution Strategy emerges from collaboration across design, product, engineering, and business.

Frameworks that work for upstream value

The "cost of building the wrong thing" is the most persuasive frame. Jared Spool's "$300 Million Button": replacing a mandatory registration form with "Continue as Guest" generated $15 million in additional revenue in the first month and $300 million in the first year.

Dan Ramsden's "confidence as currency" framework reframes discovery's output as increased organizational confidence. Counterfactual storytelling: "Without this research, we would have built Feature X ($500K cost). Research showed it wasn't needed. We redirected to Feature Y, which drove $2M in new revenue."

Service design's measurement approach

Build measurement into service blueprints from the start. Assign metrics to every frontstage and backstage action. Track operational metrics (customer effort score, cost to serve) and equity metrics (CLV, retention, repurchase intent).

Section 06

How AI and the 2023–2025 crisis are reshaping the conversation

The layoff wake-up call

Tens of thousands of design roles were eliminated. InVision shut down in January 2024. Yet companies retaining design investment outperformed: American Express saw 21% YoY net income growth; Forrester found organizations cutting design experienced 20%+ productivity declines.

Peter Merholz's 2025 survey of 763 UX/design professionals: individuals feel fulfilled, but organizations aren't supporting them. His "Design is Too Damn Big" essay argues design has no clean, bounded definition of its value.

AI's acceleration of the upstream shift

A 2025 survey of 400+ designers: 89% say AI improved their workflow, heaviest in exploration (84%) not delivery (39%). John Maeda's 2025 report introduced UX → AX (Agent Experience).

Section 07

Conclusion to Part I: a practical synthesis

The evidence base for design's business value is overwhelming. The challenge was never whether design creates value; it was that design teams lack the shared language, standardized metrics, and organizational positioning to make that value legible.

  1. Lead with pain, not capability Show the cost of bad design before arguing for the value of good design.
  2. Build causal chains Link design activities to business outcomes through leading and lagging indicators.
  3. Adopt the QA playbook Frame design investment as defect prevention (Cost of Quality model: 10–100x escalation).
  4. Create your "DORA for Design" Four to five standardized metrics covering speed and quality.
  5. Document the counterfactual The wrong product not built, the failed strategy avoided, the resources redirected.

Section 08

Where design sits in the org chart determines everything

Merholz's 2025 research across 750 UX practitioners: the more senior your head of design, the healthier your organization. He recommends the head of design sit no more than two levels from the CEO.

When design reports into a non-strategic function, "its value is seen in execution and output, not definition and outcomes." Design is systematically under-leveled relative to product and engineering: "You'll have a VP of Design, but their peers will be SVPs."

Merholz and Skinner's Org Design for Design Orgs identifies three models. The centralized services model creates disempowered designers "brought in too late." The embedded model gives proximity but isolation. Their recommended centralized partnership combines a distinct design team with committed connections to product teams.

The CDO movement

Roughly 548 companies worldwide now have a Chief Design Officer. Duolingo's CDO helped grow design to 14% of workforce, coinciding with daily active users jumping 54% in Q3 2024. JPMorgan Chase's CDO describes the progression: starting with a small mandate, demonstrating value, then earning a strategic seat.

Cultural dynamics

Kim Goodwin diagnoses company culture like a user research study, then aligns design's language with existing organizational values. Ryan Rumsey (author of Business Thinking for Designers) identifies the central gap: "Design education rarely addresses the business and political realities that determine whether design work creates actual value."

Merholz diagnoses learned helplessness among design leaders: "Many need to overcome a learned helplessness acquired over the last 20 years," calling out teams with a victim mindset who wait to be told what to do.

Section 09

Design's invisible contributions carry enormous weight

Design systems accelerate development

A Sparkbox study using IBM's Carbon design system: developers built a form page 47% faster than from scratch. Aggregated data: 38% design efficiency gains, 31% development gains. Lloyds Banking Group saved £190,000 per project, totaling £3.5M+. Qonto's design system reduced component code from 70+ lines to 21.

Research prevents catastrophic product failures

Jared Spool's $300M button: 160,000 daily password reset requests. Replacing "Register" with "Continue" yielded 45% increase in purchases, $300M in annual revenue. On the failure side: the NHS wasted £12 billion on a patient records system scrapped after nine years.

Alignment costs are quantifiable

UXPin estimates a 10-person team loses $58,500 per month to misalignment-driven inefficiencies. Design artifacts (prototypes, journey maps, blueprints) function as alignment infrastructure. Engineering effort can be reduced by roughly 50% through collaborative design tools.

Design debt compounds silently

Changying Zheng's framework describes design debt as "the accumulated cost of design decisions that prioritize short-term delivery over long-term user experience quality." Unlike technical debt, it "doesn't break builds or crash servers," but erodes experience through inconsistent UI patterns, UX friction, and accessibility shortcuts. Andrew Chen calls this what happens when features pile up without design refactoring.

"'We don't have time to do research' is usually a synonym for 'We can't risk learning something that takes out a load-bearing myth in our organization.'"

By regularly rating things 5 or 7, conviction carries disproportionate weight when you rate something a 9. Design-led startups demonstrate existential value

Stripe spent "20x more time on design than anyone else would." Figma's FigJam was built with just one PM, one developer, one designer. Canva's design-as-mission reached a $42 billion valuation with 170 million users.

Lightweight measurement frameworks

Section 11

DesignOps builds the infrastructure for measurement

DesignOps creates the conditions, systems, and baselines required to measure design's impact at all. Without it, design value remains anecdotal.

The REACH framework

Nielsen Norman Group's REACH: Results (satisfaction, success/error rates), Efficiency (operational vs. maker time ratio), Ability (existing vs. needed skills), Clarity (% of projects including design), Health (eNPS, attrition). They recommend triangulation when no single metric proves impact statistically.

DesignOps at scale

Airbnb pioneered dedicated DesignOps in 2015 with five specialized teams. Pinterest grew from 10 to 75 designers (650% growth, 2014–2017); Head of DesignOps Meredith Black defines it as "everything but actual design." Dropbox's Collin Whitehead: "The job is to protect the time and headspace of everyone within design."

Design system health metrics

Teams track: UI coverage (one team grew from ~50% to ~80% system component coverage), component usage analytics, pattern consistency scores, accessibility compliance rates, CSS class reduction, and component request volume.

Patrizia Bertini emphasizes: "Because of design's cross-functional nature, solving the design team's inefficiencies will create efficiencies across the organization."

Section 12

Design investment is risk insurance, not just ROI

A more powerful frame than ROI, especially in regulated industries: design as risk reduction.

Aviation · Safety-Critical Design Failure

Boeing 737 MAX

Two crashes killed 346 people because the MCAS system relied on a single sensor with no redundancy and wasn't disclosed to pilots. The Human Factors and Ergonomics Society testified the problems were "both knowable and entirely avoidable." The root cause was a design decision: masking an aerodynamic flaw with software to avoid expensive pilot retraining.

$20B+direct costs
$87Binvestor losses
20 monthsgrounding duration

Medical device design failures

U.S. medical device recalls tripled from 2003–2017, averaging 2,500+ per year. UI software errors accounted for 46% of software-caused recalls. In one documented case, $500K in human factors work was denied; the resulting recall of 10,000 units cost $10 million, a 20:1 ratio.

Accessibility litigation

Over 25,000 lawsuits with digital accessibility complaints filed between 2018–2025. Annual filings exceed 4,000+ per year since 2021, with a 37% increase in the first half of 2025. 77% of 2023 lawsuits targeted companies under $25 million in revenue. A single lawsuit costs $50,000–$150,000. The Domino's case (Robles v. Domino's, 2016–2022): five years of litigation when proactive accessibility investment would have cost a fraction.

The actuarial frame

(Probability of design-related failure) × (cost of that failure) = expected loss that design investment mitigates. This is the same calculation insurance companies use, and it bypasses the ROI debate entirely.

Section 13

Narrative and ritual matter more than dashboards

The most effective design leaders build organizational habits that make design's contribution continuously visible.

Structured storytelling

Shah-Brandenburg advocates a three-act structure: define the opportunity (frame the problem), outline the approach (show how design addresses it), drive action (specific asks). Her key insight: "Design is the only function providing storytelling that ties together desirability, feasibility, and viability."

"Lighthouse projects" create disproportionate visibility

Arkadiusz Radek identifies four traits: high visibility, measurable impact, strong narrative, cross-functional collaboration. His advice: replace "font family, design system, daily active users" with "growth, revenue, efficiency, risk" when speaking to executives.

Show-and-tell rituals

IDEO's Matt Cooper-Wright: "Consistency is key. Commit to running it at least four times before making changes." Flipboard's "Mock O'Clock" convenes the team every Friday. Zalando created a permanent "Inspiration Gallery" and catalogued "productivity rituals" and "happiness rituals."

Julie Zhuo's expansion of scope

Julie Zhuo frames design communication as expansion: "Maybe you start off designing a screen that converts, then a feature that enables, then an app that retains, then an experience that delights, then a business that sustains, then a way of life that fulfills."

Shah-Brandenburg offers a meta-metric for storytelling effectiveness: "Engagement can be measured by how well adjacent teams internalize and advocate for the initiative."

Section 14

How design value measurement goes wrong

Dashboard theater

Shah-Brandenburg describes a recognizable scene: a dashboard "filled with adoption rates someone once fought to establish, usability scores that once saved a launch. Someone still updates it weekly. No one questions it." When leadership sees identical UX scores monthly and nothing changes, "it's organizational theater, not strategic insight."

Anti-pattern checklist

Vanity trap: Metrics that look impressive but offer no insight (total users, app downloads). Fix: convert raw numbers into rates.

Silo trap: Metrics chosen by UX in isolation without stakeholder buy-in. "No one outside UX cares about them."

Activity vs. outcomes: Measuring component library usage and design reviews completed rather than user impact.

Individual productivity: Tasks completed per designer. "Harms collaboration" and misses team-level outcomes.

Jared Spool's critique

In "Is Design Metrically Opposed?" he demonstrated that the number of letter E's on a homepage correlates with company valuation. His Wells Fargo example: log files showed massive empty search strings, and four different teams drew four completely different inferences from the same data, each leading to different design decisions.

Peter Merholz's assessment

Merholz argues no canonical metrics exist: "The nature of UX/Design's impact is specific to the company, the audience, and the problem space." LinkedIn's DPH Framework asks: "If this metric shows you something different than you expect, will you change your course of action? If the answer is no, you shouldn't implement the metric."

The paradox of proof: the more design teams focus on proving value through metrics, the more they risk reducing design to only what's measurable, losing the qualitative, strategic, and human dimensions that make design valuable in the first place.

Section 15

Design and product management must co-own outcomes

The product trio model

NN/g's 2025 analysis debunks a misconception: that designers own "desirable," PMs own "viable," engineers own "feasible." "In reality, effective triads collaborate on achieving all three aspects." Teresa Torres: "Designating one person as the 'voice of the customer' gives that person too much power."

John Cutler's North Star Framework

Developed at Amplitude: a single metric capturing core customer value plus key inputs the team can influence. Without a shared model, Cutler observed, designers retreat to "journeys, scenarios, JTBD" while "product does its businessy thing over there." His warning about metrics theater: "The person with real domain understanding is at the back pulling their hair out. That is success theater."

Julie Zhuo on shared ownership

Zhuo: "You shouldn't just own the goals of your design. You should own the whole of the thing that your team is building. Together." She urges designers to adopt PM habits: frame every design with problem, success metric, and why your solution works.

Structural dynamics

NN/g research found "wide misalignment between PM and UX" on activity ownership. The practical bridge: not "this change will make navigation easier" but "this could reduce drop-off by 30%." The evolving model adds data fluency as a fourth pillar. Booking.com found that involving engineers in user interviews dramatically improved technical solution quality.

Section 16

Behavioral economics explains why executives undervalue design

The undervaluation of design is a predictable pattern of cognitive bias.

Liedtka's seminal research

Jeanne Liedtka's 2015 paper identifies ten cognitive biases design thinking counteracts: projection bias, egocentric empathy gap, focusing illusion, planning fallacy, endowment effect, status quo bias, availability bias, anchoring, and confirmation bias. Design thinking's structured methods function as debiasing interventions at the individual cognitive level.

System 1 / System 2 and design

Kannengiesser and Gero's research found expert designers integrate System 1 (intuitive) and System 2 (analytical) processes three times more productively than novices. Executives skipping design input default to System 1: fast, biased, pattern-matching thinking.

Four biases driving undervaluation

  1. Availability heuristic Code is visible and tangible; prevented problems and reframed strategies are not. Engineering gets credit; design's contributions remain hidden.
  2. IKEA effect Leaders overvalue features they helped ideate over solutions from design research they didn't participate in.
  3. Status quo bias Organizations that historically invested in engineering continue that pattern regardless of evidence for design investment.
  4. Tangibility bias People struggle to assess abstract process outputs (problem framing, user insights, strategic direction) compared to concrete deliverables.

The prevention paradox

Originally described by Geoffrey Rose in public health, it applies precisely to design: a problem occurs, the organization invests in design, research prevents future problems, the absence of problems makes the investment appear unnecessary, the budget is cut, problems recur. Harvard's Fineberg (2013, JAMA): "The success of prevention is invisible, lacks drama... prevention is expected to produce a net financial return, whereas treatment is expected only to be worth its cost."

Section 17

Emerging measurement tools and frameworks (2024–2026)

The TARS framework

Adrian Raudaschl's 2025 framework (Smashing Magazine): Target Audience % (what share of users have the problem?), Adoption rate, Retention rate, Satisfaction via Customer Effort Score. The derived S÷T Score maps features onto a 2×2 matrix of core, overperforming, liability, and project features.

AI-powered analytics

Figma's Config 2025: Figma Make (AI-powered prototype generation from text prompts), AI assistant across the entire design workflow, and universal search across files and usage metrics. Dylan Field: "In a world where AI makes it easier than ever to build software, design will become more essential and powerful."

Quantum Metric's Felix AI deploys autonomous agents analyzing every part of the customer journey, automatically capturing 300+ metrics and calculating the revenue impact of every customer behavior and friction point. Named a Strong Performer in Forrester's 2025 Wave.

The post-NPS movement

Gartner predicted 75%+ of organizations would abandon NPS by 2025. Emerging replacements: Customer Effort Score, Experience Quality Index, MeasuringU's UX-Lite with new grading scales, and Pendo's Product Engagement Score combining breadth, depth, and frequency.

Design teams gaining data autonomy

IBM's adoption of Amplitude for self-serve design analytics: 980% increase in platform usage among UX designers, with a 20% reduction in conversion time. Figma's 2025 AI Report: 85% of designers say learning AI is essential, 52% of AI builders say design is more important for AI products.

Section 18

20 practitioner articles on proving design's value (annotated reading list)

The most honest writing about design's value problem comes not from research firms or consultancies, but from individual practitioners who have lived it. The 20 articles below span personal blogs, Medium, Substack, and newsletters from leaders at companies like Microsoft, Eventbrite, Asana, Facebook, and Badoo. Three patterns cut across the entire collection: designers have largely created their own credibility gap, the most effective practitioners tie design metrics to existing business metrics rather than inventing new ones, and design leaders who excel at craft often fail at the strategic advocacy and cross-functional politics that leadership demands.

Failure stories and critiques

ROI and the ego trap

Scott Berkun · Former design leader at Microsoft and WordPress.com · Feb 2025 · Substack

Berkun dismantles the design community's favorite ROI arguments, calling them an "ego trap." He critiques the Design Value Index as a biased, correlation-not-causation study. His central argument: executives don't evaluate design ROI in the abstract; they compare it against alternative investments like marketing or engineering. Designers who blame CEOs for not "getting it" are abdicating responsibility.

Key takeaways
  • Executives invest by comparison. Design must prove it's a better investment than alternatives for this specific business, not in the abstract
  • The "Cassandra complex" (complaining about being ignored) is an admission of helplessness, not a strategy
  • Designers should apply design thinking to their own organizational frustration

The mistakes we make in making the case for design

Amanda Linden · Design leader (Designer Fund) · ~2017 · Medium

Linden admits she was rarely the one convincing companies to invest in design. She identifies the biggest mistake designers make: advocating for great design ideas that don't align with current company priorities. She argues the vast majority of time should be spent socializing a design vision, not developing the design itself.

Key takeaways
  • A good design idea that doesn't match company priorities will die regardless of its merit
  • Spend most time socializing the vision, not perfecting the design
  • Think like a business leader: expect messiness, don't take pushback personally

How design success becomes leadership failure

Ken Sigel · Product Design Leader (Boston) · Jul 2025 · Medium

Sigel argues the skills that make designers great individual contributors become liabilities at the executive level. He identifies three critical gaps: speaking design while peers speak business, staying insular instead of building cross-functional alliances, and defaulting to execution work while avoiding strategic advocacy.

Key takeaways
  • Three critical gaps: language (UX vs. revenue), alliance (insular vs. cross-functional), work (execution vs. strategy)
  • "When you reach senior leadership you're playing an entirely different game with different rules"
  • Freedom becomes a trap: no one tells a design director how to spend their time, so they default to familiar craft work

How to survive the design leadership reckoning

Martyn Reding · Design leader and speaker · 2024 · Medium

Reding argues that "design leadership as we know it is over," tracing the arc from the design thinking boom through hiring surges to the current reckoning. He notes that 70%+ of advertised design leadership roles now require craft skills and a portfolio, and that IDEO's decline symbolizes broader disillusionment. The double diamond framework is now "triggering for stakeholders."

Key takeaways
  • Design leadership has entered a hype-cycle "trough of disillusionment"
  • Specialist roles have collapsed into generalist positions as teams shrank
  • Adaptation requires letting go of firmly held beliefs from the growth era, not doubling down on them

Design is hard. Design leadership is harder.

Jason Mesut · Design leader, Leading Design speaker · ~2018 · Medium

A deeply personal reflection on Mesut's biggest leadership failure: creating a team too dependent on him as a single leader. When he left, the team's stability and direction collapsed. He writes about attending conferences where "confident, famous, and successful leaders" openly struggle with the same challenges.

Key takeaways
  • Creating a team reliant on a single leader for stability and direction is itself a leadership failure
  • Formal references are "next to useless"; personal references are essential for hiring
  • "Our relationships will last longer than the project, or the product. Longer than our current jobs."
Frameworks practitioners built to measure impact

How to explain the value of design to your boss in 5 steps

Paul Boag · Independent UX consultant (32 years in digital; Puma, UNICEF, Oxford) · Mar 2020

Boag argues the problem isn't a lack of evidence for design's value but how designers communicate that evidence. He references Jared Spool's insight that "you can never convince executives of anything" and proposes meeting them where they already are. His five-step framework shifts the burden of proof onto designers.

Key takeaways
  • 5-step framework: (1) Recognize it's YOUR responsibility, (2) Know what tangible change you want, (3) Understand your audience's priorities, (4) Start small with evidence, (5) Never give up
  • Frame design differently for each stakeholder: cost efficiencies for CFOs, brand loyalty for marketing, competitive advantage for CEOs
  • Don't ask for a full-time researcher on day one. Run one small usability test first and let results speak

How to quantify the value of design

Paul Batterham · Head of Design at Kainos · ~2022 · Medium

After 20+ years of being asked to "colour in wireframes," Batterham developed a framework for articulating design's quantifiable value at the project level. The model uses a user-story-style template: "As a [role], I did [activity], which resulted in [outcome], delivering [benefit], measured by [metric]." The framework can also be inverted: teams needing specific benefits can work backwards to identify the right design activity.

Key takeaways
  • Activity → Outcome → Benefit → Measure framework: four layers mapping specific design activities to quantifiable results
  • Inversible: can be used forward (what value does this activity create?) or backward (we need this benefit; what activity achieves it?)
  • "If designers are not responsible for articulating the value of their work, who is?"

How to measure design impact

Tanner Christensen · Designer at Gem; prev. Facebook, Lyft, Atlassian · Jun 2021

Christensen walks through his hands-on methodology combining "Top Tasks" analysis with PURE (Pragmatic Usability Ratings by Experts). The 6-step process includes mapping user job stages, surveying both internal experts and customers on perceived pain, creating a color-coded pain index, and conducting PURE analysis. Scores are re-evaluated quarterly to track improvement.

Key takeaways
  • Top Tasks + PURE combined framework: identify painful user jobs via pain-rating workshops, then score with Pragmatic Usability Ratings (ease, speed, efficiency on 1–3 scale)
  • Pain Index creates a visual, color-coded priority system that non-designers immediately understand
  • Quarterly measurement cadence makes improvement undeniable (e.g., total score dropping from 60 to 52)

Beyond vanity metrics: a three-lens framework for measuring design impact

Twisha Shah-Brandenburg · Author of "Designing for Enterprise Impact" · Apr 2025

Shah-Brandenburg tackles the paradox that design's most valuable contributions (trust, friction reduction, emotional connection) are the hardest to measure. She proposes a Three-Lens Framework and identifies "Five Deadly Sins" of design measurement, including the Executive Summary Trap and the Usability Success Ceiling.

Key takeaways
  • Three-Lens Framework: (1) Behavioral Metrics ("Can users succeed?"), (2) Strategic Metrics ("Are we driving business outcomes?"), (3) Emotional Metrics ("How do we make people feel?")
  • Define "decision thresholds" upfront: what metric changes trigger specific actions
  • Monthly audits against three lenses; quarterly decision-making framework reviews; annual measurement literacy building

A trinity of metrics for measuring design's business impact

Esko Lehtme · Design leader (Estonia, financial services) · May 2025

Lehtme developed a measurement system mapping three categories: customer experience, technical performance, and business value, across each phase of the customer journey. He discovered that usability tests sometimes showed solid results even when live usage dropped, revealing the gap between controlled testing and real-world behavior.

Key takeaways
  • Trinity of Metrics: Customer Experience (CSAT by phase), Technical Performance (completion times, error rates), Business Value (completion rates, new sales, CLV)
  • Customer journey as organizing principle: structure measurement around 6 phases (Trigger, Discovery, Evaluation, Trial, Usage, Decision)
  • Translate drop-offs at each journey phase into financial losses: priorities shift from subjective preferences to strategic imperatives

Calculating the ROI of UX: a 5-step framework

Trevor Calabro · Principal UX Researcher (18 years experience) · Jan 2025 (updated Aug 2025)

The most calculation-heavy framework in this collection, with two real-world examples: a university registration form redesign yielding 74.5% ROI ($14,900 net annual benefit) and a file export workflow redesign yielding 882.7% ROI ($6.6M net annual benefit). He converts UX metrics into dollars using simple formulas.

Key takeaways
  • 5-step ROI: (1) Identify problem and benchmarks, (2) Define goals, (3) Gather data to estimate ROI, (4) Calculate using (Return − Investment) / Investment, (5) Communicate results
  • Convert UX metrics to money: time savings × hourly rate = cost savings; error reduction × support cost = operational savings
  • Five common pitfalls: overestimating ROI, focusing only on direct benefits, ignoring long-term impact, failing to account for total costs, poor communication

Measuring the impact of a design system

Cristiano Rastelli · Design Systems Lead at Badoo (now Bumble Group) · ~2019 · Medium

Rastelli shares his creative solution to an intractable measurement problem. Qualitative feedback on Badoo's Cosmos design system was positive ("everything now is like LEGO") but couldn't be converted to numbers. His breakthrough: analyzing git log data to visualize code change frequency before and after design system adoption, showing dramatic reduction in UI-related code churn.

Key takeaways
  • Git log analysis as a design system metric: track commit frequency and volume in UI-related files to show reduced rework
  • Surveys capture sentiment but can't prove causation; version control data provides objective evidence
  • Frame value as "work better, not less": reduce repetitive work so people focus on important problems
Company-specific case studies and personal accounts

Prove your worth by connecting UX to revenue

Sally Grisedale (featuring Anshuman Kumar) · Former UX leader at Apple, Yahoo!, Meta; Kumar: former SVP/Head of Design, Asana · Oct 2025 · Substack

The most concrete case study in the collection. Kumar adopted Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) as his design team's North Star at Asana, reframed UX bugs as revenue opportunities, and generated $10 million in ARR in one year. He audited high-value UX surfaces, created a priority framework, and built a public leaderboard tracking experiments and their revenue impact.

Key takeaways
  • Align design KPIs to three business areas: financial performance (conversion, AOV), user growth (DAU/MAU), customer retention (CSAT, churn)
  • Pick a North Star project goal that is unambiguous, measurable, and time-bound
  • Gamify the results: Kumar built a public leaderboard tracking experiments and their ARR impact, creating organizational excitement

How to communicate the ROI of design

Brian Beaver (VP of Design, Eventbrite) & Susan Dybbs (Head of Design, Collective Health) · Nov 2016 · Designer Fund

Beaver describes building an ROI model to justify Eventbrite's atomic design system by partnering with finance and marketing to quantify efficiency savings. He coined the term "design debt" (modeled after "engineering debt") to instantly communicate tradeoffs. Dybbs explains how Collective Health co-owns key company metrics (NPS, CSAT) and shares them biweekly with leadership.

Key takeaways
  • Co-opt engineering's "tech debt" as "design debt" to unlock conversations: "When I say this in a meeting, people instantly get it"
  • Partner with finance and marketing to translate design benefits into dollars: "it's like a multiplier effect"
  • Design should co-own company metrics and share biweekly, pairing numbers with qualitative color (customer quotes, videos)

How to prove the value of design to your CEO

Chris Novak (Microsoft/Xbox), Melissa Cullens (Ellevest), August De Los Reyes (Varo Money), Ryan Rumsey (Apple/EA), Alen Faljic (d.MBA) · Mar 2020 · Abstract

Prompted by McKinsey's finding that 66% of CEOs couldn't say what their CDO actually did, six design leaders share personal perspectives. Novak emphasizes mapping design sponsorship before advocacy. Cullens admits "we have not finished the work of translating value into measurable, objective outcomes." Faljic argues design education has "completely let down a whole generation."

Key takeaways
  • Map design sponsorship first: identify who has hire/fire/budget authority before beginning advocacy (Novak)
  • Adopt a service leadership mindset for peers: solve internal problems for colleagues to demonstrate design's capabilities (Cullens)

Proving the value of design (Part 1)

Scott Faranello · Design practitioner/leader · Medium

Faranello argues the confusion about design's business value was "largely brought about by designers themselves." For decades, designers worked in a qualitative environment, trying to convince executives of design's subjective value "as if it was some 'magic flute.'" He critiques the common approach of showing infographics from Apple and Airbnb while failing to prove value within one's own organization.

Key takeaways
  • Self-inflicted problem: designers created the confusion by operating in purely qualitative environments and ignoring quantitative proof
  • Strategy-first approach: isolated usability studies and A/B tests won't tell the full story without a connective strategy
  • The biggest misconception: UX has been reduced to knowing where to place "Buy" buttons, undermining strategic potential
Leadership reflections on organizational value

The difference between a VP of design and a VP of anything else

Jehad Affoneh · VP of Design · Oct 2021 · Personal blog

Affoneh identifies four structural differences that make design leadership uniquely challenging. His central insight: "The number of hours of advocacy that it takes to prove the value of design becomes a core part of the role without a clear and expected return on investment." He describes being the single design leader surrounded by multiple PM and engineering VPs.

Key takeaways
  • Four structural differences: clarity of role, depth of ownership (one design VP vs. many PM/eng VPs), stakeholder burden, operational groundwork
  • Moving goalposts: what proved value last year becomes table stakes this year
  • The structural disadvantages are also advantages: breadth of knowledge across all verticals and ability to connect dots no other leader can

The makeup of a design executive

Peter Merholz · Design leadership consultant, co-author of Org Design for Design Orgs · Dec 2020 (updated Apr 2025)

From consulting work helping companies hire design executives, Merholz finds organizations have a "rudimentary understanding" of the role, focused narrowly on "being a creative leader." He developed a four-part framework: Executive (33%), Creative (32%), People (20%), Operational (15%). Without this breakdown, People and Operational demands consume all time.

Key takeaways
  • Four-part design executive framework: Executive (company strategy, cross-org advocacy), Creative (quality, direction), People (recruiting, careers), Operational (process, budgets, DesignOps)
  • Diagnostic use: when People and Ops consume all time, use the framework to justify hiring a Head of DesignOps
  • Without intentional time allocation, the Executive and Creative work (the work that proves design's value) never happens

The future of design leadership

Tey Bannerman · Former leader of McKinsey's European design practice (writing independently) · May 2025 · Personal blog

Bannerman writes from experience as "the first designer they'd ever met at this level" in executive boardrooms. He proposes four critical evolutions for design leaders: from competitive differentiation to co-creation, from performance expectations to human wholeness, from command to collaboration, and from measuring outcomes to enabling impact. Only four behaviors account for 89% of leadership effectiveness.

Key takeaways
  • Shift from "What did we deliver?" to "What did we enable?" as the measurement frame
  • Five battle-tested tactics: 30-day design sprints on business processes, fortnightly dialogue between design and finance, the "10X vs. 10%" decision framework
  • The evolving design leader DNA: Manager→Human, Planner→Catalyst, Professional→Visionary, Director→Architect

7 problems growing design teams face

Aarron Walter · VP of Design Education at InVision; founder of UX practice at MailChimp; author of Designing for Emotion · Jun 2016 · Medium

Walter identifies seven systemic problems from nearly a decade of leading product teams. He describes the fundamental tension where designers need both design community and cross-functional collaboration, but most organizational structures force them to choose. Includes examples from Slack, Spotify, Airbnb, and IBM, and quotes Julie Zhuo: "The graph of impact tends to correlate with how many people you need to work effectively with."

What these 20 articles reveal in aggregate
Pattern 1: Self-inflicted credibility gap

The most repeated insight across this collection is that designers have created their own credibility problem by operating in qualitative environments, speaking design jargon instead of business language, and expecting their work to "speak for itself" (Berkun, Faranello, Faljic, Boag, Sigel).

Pattern 2: Tie to existing business metrics

The most effective practitioners all tied design metrics directly to existing business metrics rather than inventing new ones: ARR at Asana, NPS at Collective Health, code churn at Badoo, completion rates in financial services (Kumar, Dybbs, Rastelli, Lehtme).

Pattern 3: Craft success, leadership failure

The failure stories share a common thread: design leaders who succeeded at craft failed at politics, defaulting to familiar execution work while neglecting the strategic advocacy and cross-functional relationship-building that leadership demands (Sigel, Mesut, Affoneh, Merholz).

The practitioner frameworks cluster into two camps. The measurement-first camp (Calabro, Christensen, Shah-Brandenburg, Lehtme, Batterham) builds systems for quantifying impact with specific metrics and cadences. The communication-first camp (Boag, Linden, Beaver, Cullens) focuses on how to frame and socialize design's value to different audiences. The most successful practitioners, like Kumar at Asana and Dybbs at Collective Health, combined both: rigorous measurement translated into language each stakeholder already cared about.

Conclusion

Design value is proven through structure, story, and systems

Proving design's value is itself a design problem: one requiring structural positioning, narrative craft, operational infrastructure, and cognitive awareness, not just better metrics.

  1. Position design structurally near power Merholz's data: where design reports determines how it's valued. No amount of dashboarding overcomes a bad org chart.
  2. Build DesignOps infrastructure Make invisible work visible. Track design system adoption, design debt, and alignment costs.
  3. Use narrative and ritual Lighthouse projects, show-and-tells, structured storytelling. Consistency builds cumulative credibility.
  4. Frame as risk insurance where appropriate Boeing ($20B+ losses), medical device recalls (20:1 cost ratio), accessibility lawsuits (4,000+ annually). The actuarial frame bypasses the ROI debate.
  5. Co-own outcomes with product management Shared North Star Metrics, product trios, joint OKR ownership. Not "desirable vs. viable vs. feasible" but all three together.
  6. Understand the cognitive biases working against you The prevention paradox, availability heuristic, tangibility bias, and IKEA effect systematically devalue design. Name them to neutralize them.
  7. Measure what matters, not what's easy No universal metric exists. Effective measurement is always specific to the company, audience, and problem space. If a metric wouldn't change your course of action, don't implement it.
The bottom line

The profession's future depends not on whether design is valuable, but on whether designers can prove it in the language their organizations already speak. Liedtka's research provides the academic anchor: design thinking functions as a structured debiasing intervention, systematically improving decision quality. The practitioner voices in Part III confirm this is not abstract theory: Kumar generated $10M in ARR at Asana by adopting revenue as design's North Star, Beaver unlocked conversations at Eventbrite by coining "design debt," and Rastelli at Badoo used git log data as objective proof. The practical response combines all eighteen sections of this report: organizational structure, measurement frameworks, cross-domain analogies, risk framing, narrative strategy, practitioner wisdom, and an honest reckoning with what measurement can and cannot do.
