Our own frameworks for rapid experimentation

Through our work building a creative technology capability and running experimental builds with government partners, we've developed frameworks essential for making this work in practice.

The delivery paradox. When we test something and it fails, we've prevented wasted investment. That's a good outcome. But the partner sees: no deliverable, nothing shipped. It feels like failure. Solving this requires reframing success before the experiment starts.

The frozen lake principle. If a kid wants to get across a frozen lake and starts running and slips, that's not the end. They've learned something: maybe running is the problem, or maybe they've learned something about the lake itself. The first failure doesn't mean you can't cross.

Two categories of work. (1) "Just build it" for lower-risk areas where partners are SMEs and own outcomes. (2) "Test the crucial assumptions" for high-risk areas with the biggest unknowns. Our expertise is knowing which is which.

"Experimental builds" not "prototypes." When we say "prototype," people think Figma mockup. What we mean is working builds that aren't meant to scale and might break, but get something real into users' hands.

Setting up for failure. Build iteration pathways upfront, not just single tests. Have a clear next move before you start. The value isn't just disproving bad ideas quickly. It's having a clear direction for what comes after.

Where we are now is interim. The playbook needs a future outlook.
Phase 1 / Now

Learning AI

Teams getting familiar with tools. Excitement, experimentation, but still bolting AI onto existing ways of working. This is where most government teams are today.

Phase 2 / Near-term

Designing workflows with AI

Deliberately mapping which parts get augmented, replaced, or are entirely new. Not "I used AI faster" but rethinking the workflow itself.

Phase 3 / The shift

Designing for AI as a medium

What does a service look like when an AI agent is the user? What does a form look like as a conversation? This is designing FOR AI, not just WITH AI.

Seven tensions to navigate honestly

Speed vs. quality

78% say AI enhances efficiency, but only 32% trust output. Define when speed matters vs. when quality matters.

Generalist vs. specialist

Startups hire generalists. Big tech compresses specialist tracks. The "full-stack builder" risks losing deep expertise.

Upskilling vs. replacing

Microsoft trains everyone. Accenture exits those who can't reskill. Which is right depends on your context and values.

Efficiency vs. capability

93% of tech spending goes to technology, 7% to training. This ratio undermines long-term capability.

Open vs. silent AI

Branded AI triggers backlash. Invisible AI gets loved. Best AI design is often the kind users don't notice.

Delivery paradox

When an experiment fails, we've prevented waste. But partners see no deliverable. Reframe success before experiments start.

Five commitments for the playbook

1

We solve the right problems.

Upstream discovery is our highest-value activity. We validate before we build.

2

We raise design quality across the organization.

Design is a capability we steward. We set standards and equip others.

3

We experiment rapidly and learn publicly.

We build experimental builds at AI speed. We share what we learn, including failures.

4

We use AI as leverage, not replacement for judgment.

We maintain quality gates and human review for citizen-facing work.

5

We build the next generation.

We maintain junior pathways and invest in structured learning.
